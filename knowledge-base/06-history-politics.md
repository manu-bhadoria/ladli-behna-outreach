# 06 — History & Political Context

## Origin

Launched by **CM Shivraj Singh Chouhan (BJP)** — announced 28 January 2023, operationalised 5 March 2023, first money credited June 2023. The scheme is part of Chouhan's long-running welfare brand built on "*Mamaji*" (maternal-uncle) positioning, following earlier schemes like Ladli Laxmi Yojana (2007, for girl children) and Mukhyamantri Kanya Vivah Yojana.

## The 2023 election effect

The 2023 Madhya Pradesh Assembly election (held November 2023) is widely read as the first clean test of the scheme's electoral weight.

- **Result:** BJP won 163 of 230 seats — a much larger margin than most pre-poll surveys had predicted.
- **Women's vote share:** ~50% of women voters backed BJP — about **10 percentage points higher than Congress's women's share**, and **6 points higher than BJP's own male vote share** in the same state.
- **Post-election framing:** Senior BJP leaders including Jyotiraditya Scindia publicly credited the scheme as the "game-changer." Most political commentators concurred, making Ladli Behna a reference point that other states (MH, JH, WB) have since tried to replicate.

## Transition to CM Mohan Yadav

Despite the BJP's emphatic win, Shivraj Singh Chouhan was **not** retained as CM. **Mohan Yadav** was appointed CM in December 2023. He inherited the scheme and has continued it with:
- Hike from ₹1,250 → ₹1,500 (Diwali 2024)
- Public commitment to reach ₹3,000/month by 2028
- Festival bonuses (Rakhi Shagun ₹250 in 2024 and subsequently)

## Cross-state imitation

Ladli Behna's electoral success has been consciously copied:

| State | Scheme | Launched | Amount |
|---|---|---|---|
| Maharashtra | **Mukhyamantri Majhi Ladki Bahin Yojana** | 2024 | ₹1,500/month |
| Jharkhand | **Maiyya Samman Yojana** | 2024 | ₹1,000 → ₹2,500/month |
| West Bengal | **Lakshmir Bhandar** (predates MP) | 2021 | ₹1,000 – ₹1,200/month |
| Delhi | **Mukhyamantri Mahila Samman Yojana** | 2025 (promised) | ₹2,100/month |

These schemes share a common architecture — direct cash, monthly, to women — but differ in eligibility cutoffs, caste/category targeting, and amounts.

**Do not conflate MP's Ladli Behna with Maharashtra's Ladki Bahin.** They sound similar but are administered by different state governments, have different eligibility rules, and different Samagra-equivalent databases.

## Fiscal debate

The scheme has drawn two main lines of critique:
1. **Fiscal sustainability** — state outlay ≈ 7–8% of state budget and rising; critics argue it crowds out capex. Supporters argue demand-side stimulus via women's accounts has stronger multiplier effects than equivalent capex in rural MP.
2. **Targeting precision** — the ₹2.5 lakh family-income ceiling is high; some economists argue the scheme is effectively near-universal rather than targeted, which dilutes impact per rupee.

Both debates are live and should be acknowledged factually — not dismissed — in any outreach that might face informed counter-questions.

## Controversies

- **Vijay Shah remarks (2024–25):** MP minister Vijay Shah drew sharp criticism — including a Congress demand for his resignation — for remarks perceived to tie beneficiary payments to attendance at a CM felicitation event. Shah denied intent. Useful context if outreach audiences raise it: the scheme's rules do not attach any political-attendance conditionality; payments are a statutory DBT flow.
- **Removal surges before installments:** Every few installments see a spike in removed names (age-out + verification drives). This is normal administrative cleanup, though it repeatedly generates "my mother's name was removed!" grievance coverage in local media.
