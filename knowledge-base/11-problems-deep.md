# 11 — Problems with the scheme (deeper research)

Extends `07-exclusions-grievances.md` (tactical pain points) with the **structural, dignity-level, and political-economy critiques** that don't show up in the operational FAQ. If you're designing voice outreach, these are the failure modes the call must NOT compound.

## A · Operational failures, ranked by beneficiary pain

### A1. New registrations are closed — biggest grievance right now

- Scheme was frozen to new entrants after the 2023 enrollment wave. Women who have turned 21 **since then** cannot enter.
- **February 2026:** Congress MLAs staged a walkout in the MP Assembly demanding fresh registrations. Minister Nirmala Bhuria (WCD) told the House that no proposal for new registration is currently under consideration. CM Mohan Yadav has said reopening will happen "slowly."
- **MP High Court (Feb 2026)** dismissed a PIL challenging the registration freeze, ruling it a policy decision inside the government's domain.
- Affected population: every woman in MP who crossed 21 after ~March 2023 (roughly ~50 lakh women over the 3-year freeze window, cumulative).
- **Why this matters for outreach:** Any contact with a 21–23-year-old woman should avoid implying she has an active entitlement. The scheme, for her, is a promise of a future reopening — not a live payment.

### A2. Samagra ↔ Aadhaar eKYC mismatch — silent non-payment

- Concentrated in **Satna** and **Singrauli** districts in reported grievance patterns.
- Signature: application shows *"approved, Aadhaar linked"* in the portal, but no money arrives. Beneficiary has no way of knowing the mismatch failed eKYC silently.
- Six technical reasons reported as blockers:
  1. Aadhaar name ≠ Samagra name (spelling / middle name / after-marriage change)
  2. DOB mismatch Aadhaar vs Samagra
  3. Bank account Aadhaar-seeded but **not DBT-activated** (separate step, poorly communicated)
  4. Aadhaar linked to multiple accounts — DBT routes to only the most-recent-mapped one
  5. Samagra ID deleted / deactivated in backend cleanup
  6. Bank account dormant (no transactions for 12+ months)

### A3. Age-out at 60 is hard, with no automatic transition

- ~3.04 lakh cumulative removals at age 60. No automatic enrollment into old-age pension (NSAP/IGNOAPS), state widow pension, or other safety nets.
- Women learn they've been removed only when the next installment doesn't arrive. No proactive notification from the state.

### A4. Travel cost erodes benefit

- **Harbai, 25** (Ujjain district, BehanBox 2023): spends ₹70 to reach the bank → ₹1,430 net from a ₹1,500 payment.
- **Ratibai** paid ₹1,700 for an LPG cylinder with "no idea about subsidy status" — a reminder that *adjacent* scheme confusion (LPG/Ujjwala/PDS) bleeds into the Ladli Behna experience from the woman's point of view.

### A5. Bureaucratic dead-ends

- **Shyama (Katehari village):** completed all Ujjwala registration steps, never received the gas connection, no explanation given. Not Ladli Behna specifically, but captures the typical pattern — the system collects information and then goes silent. Outreach that merely *adds one more collection point* without closing the loop reinforces the same pattern.

## B · Structural / dignity-level critiques

These are the critiques that academics, gender researchers, and frontline workers make. They don't show up in the government data; they show up in how the scheme *lands* socially.

### B1. "Behna" framing locks women into the recipient role

- The naming — *Ladli* (beloved) + *Behna* (sister) — positions the woman as a dependent female relative of the state/CM. Critics (ArmChair Journal) argue this reinforces patriarchal framing even while transferring cash.
- **ASHA worker Lakshmi Kaurav** (quoted in BehanBox): *"Instead of sisterhood we need economic empowerment and recognition of our livelihoods."* — the sharpest summary of the dignity critique from a frontline worker.

### B2. Cash without decision-making power ≠ empowerment

- Academic point: economic power is proportional to decision-making power. A monthly transfer that beneficiaries cannot influence (eligibility rules, amount, timing, delivery mechanism) positions them as *eligible* rather than *enfranchised*.
- Practical corollary: the scheme does not ask women what they want. It tells them what they'll get.

### B3. Sunset risk

- A scheme tied so tightly to one party's political fortunes creates an implicit threat: *"will this survive a change in government?"* That unspoken dependency is itself a form of coercion, even without any explicit conditionality.

### B4. Intersectional blind spots

- Documentation requirements (Aadhaar + Samagra + own bank + DBT-mapped) privilege women with paper trails. Marginalised caste and tribal women are more likely to fail on one of those four conditions and be silently excluded.
- **Anita Gond (Sidhi):** gets Ladli Behna every month. Her complaint: *"pipelines have been laid but there is no water which is a big problem."* She is ENROLLED, yet her village has no water. The scheme delivers cash against a backdrop of broken infrastructure.

### B5. Money-scale mismatch

- ₹1,500/month = ~₹18,000/year. The scheme cannot meaningfully address violence, dowry, bride-burning, or structural inequities it gestures at in rhetoric. Critics hold that the mismatch between claimed purpose and actual transfer creates a credibility gap.

## C · Political weaponization — Vijay Shah and the "gratitude tax"

The single sharpest ongoing controversy is Tribal Affairs Minister **Vijay Shah's** repeated suggestions that beneficiary payments be tied to visible political loyalty.

- **December 2025 (Ratlam):** Shah told District Development Advisory Committee that Ladli Behna beneficiaries attending a felicitation programme for CM Mohan Yadav would get ₹250 extra — and those who did not attend would have their verification kept "pending."
- **May–June 2025:** Earlier round of remarks suggesting attendance-linked aid; denied after Congress backlash.
- **Congress Mahila Morcha protest** in Bhopal demanding his dismissal.
- Shah's track record: 2013 indecent remarks about Sadhna Singh (Shivraj's wife) forced an earlier resignation; 2022 mocked Rahul Gandhi's unmarried status.

**Why this matters for outreach:** A CM voice-cloned call automatically inherits the aura of Vijay Shah's remarks. If it sounds remotely like a loyalty audit — *"did you come to the rally?" / "did you vote?"* — the entire programme is tarred as attendance-for-payment. The trust cost is catastrophic and asymmetric: one bad 30-second clip going viral on WhatsApp could contaminate every subsequent outreach call.

## D · What women SAY they want, beyond the money

Direct quotes from beneficiaries — these are the sharpest signal.

- **Shantibai:** *"The scheme is good, but there are other needs...such as having access to basic facilities like roads and water."*
- **Anita Gond (Sidhi):** *"I get money under Ladli Behna Yojana every month but there needs to be some arrangement made for water supply in the village. The pipelines have been laid but there is no water which is a big problem."*
- **Sunita Bai (Budhni):** *"I was yet to receive my wages and urgently needed some cash. With money credited every month, there is some cushion to fall back on in times of crises."* (Positive quote — but note the framing: the money is a *cushion*, not transformation. It fills a cashflow gap; it does not change her life.)
- **Lakshmi Kaurav (ASHA worker):** *"Instead of sisterhood we need economic empowerment and recognition of our livelihoods."*
- **Male reaction (ThePrint quote):** *"Those men who do not earn are the ones talking against the scheme as they have to ask women for money. Some men are unhappy because the money is not going in their hands. They have to, probably for the first time, ask women when they run out of cash."*

The pattern across the quotes: women are **grateful for the cushion** but **frustrated at infrastructure gaps** — water, roads, wages, recognition. A voice call that only thanks-and-celebrates the money misreads the room. A voice call that asks *"what else is needed?"* reads it correctly.
